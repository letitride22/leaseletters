How to Serve a Late Rent Notice in Texas

Texas law (Texas Property Code Section 24.005) governs how notices must be delivered. Accepted methods include:

  • 1Personal service — Hand the notice directly to the tenant. This is the most reliable method and starts the clock immediately.
  • 2Certified mail — Send via USPS certified mail with return receipt. Keep the receipt as proof of delivery. Note that the notice period may not begin until the tenant receives it.
  • 3Posting and mailing — Post the notice in a conspicuous place on the property AND mail a copy the same day. Both steps are required for this method to be valid.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I file for eviction immediately after the 3 days?

Yes — Texas courts move quickly. Once the 3-day notice expires and rent hasn't been paid, you can file a forcible detainer suit in your local Justice of the Peace court the very next day.

Does Texas require a grace period before sending the notice?

State law does not require a grace period, but check your lease — many leases include a 1-3 day grace period before late fees apply. If your lease has one, honor it before sending the notice.

Can I include late fees in the Texas notice?

You can include late fees if your lease specifies them, but Texas courts prefer notices that state just the base rent owed. Some landlords send separate demand letters for fees to avoid complications with the eviction notice.

What if the tenant pays after receiving the notice?

If the tenant pays in full before the 3 days expire, the notice is satisfied. Accept the payment and document it. In Texas you are not required to accept partial payment — doing so can waive your right to evict for that period.
