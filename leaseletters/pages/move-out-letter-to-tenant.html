What a Move-Out Letter Should Cover

A thorough move-out letter serves as both a checklist for the tenant and a legal record for you. Include all of the following:

  • Move-out date and time
  • Key return instructions
  • Move-out inspection process
  • Cleaning requirements
  • Security deposit timeline
  • Forwarding address request
  • Utility transfer instructions
  • Parking pass / fob return

The Move-Out Timeline: What to Do and When

  • 130 days before move-out — Send the move-out letter. Give the tenant enough time to prepare, schedule cleaning, and arrange the inspection.
  • 2Move-out day — Conduct a walkthrough inspection with the tenant present if possible. Document everything with photos and video. Note existing wear vs. actual damage.
  • 3Within 24–48 hours — Send the tenant a written summary of any damage observed during the inspection, separate from the deposit itemization.
  • 4Before your state deadline — Return the deposit with an itemized statement of any deductions, or return it in full. Send via certified mail and keep receipts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I deduct cleaning costs from the security deposit?

Yes, if the unit is left in worse condition than normal wear and tear. "Normal wear and tear" means minor scuffs, small nail holes, and general aging — not deep carpet stains, broken fixtures, or walls that need repainting due to abuse. Document the original condition with your move-in checklist to protect yourself.

What if the tenant doesn't provide a forwarding address?

Send the deposit and itemization to the last known address — the rental property itself. Keep proof of the attempted delivery. In many states, if a tenant fails to provide a forwarding address, it can affect their ability to sue you for the deposit.

Does the tenant have to be present for the move-out inspection?

In some states (California, for example), you are required to offer the tenant a pre-move-out inspection where you point out issues they can fix before leaving. Final move-out inspection can proceed without them, but document everything thoroughly with photos.

Can I keep the deposit if the tenant breaks the lease early?

You can only keep the deposit for actual damages — unpaid rent for the remaining term (in states without duty to mitigate), cleaning, or repairs. You generally cannot keep it simply as a penalty for early termination unless your lease specifically and lawfully allows for it.
