Florida Late Rent Rules: What Landlords Must Know

Before sending any notice, understand Florida's specific requirements. Serving the wrong notice — or serving it incorrectly — can restart your entire eviction timeline.

Key Florida Rule: Florida law requires the notice to state the exact amount of rent due. Do not include late fees or other charges in the notice amount — it can invalidate the entire notice.

How to Serve a Late Rent Notice in Florida

Florida law (Florida Statute Section 83.56) governs how notices must be delivered. Accepted methods include:

  • 1Personal service — Hand the notice directly to the tenant. This is the most reliable method and starts the clock immediately.
  • 2Certified mail — Send via USPS certified mail with return receipt. Keep the receipt as proof of delivery. Note that the notice period may not begin until the tenant receives it.
  • 3Posting and mailing — Post the notice in a conspicuous place on the property AND mail a copy the same day. Both steps are required for this method to be valid.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I count the 3 days in Florida?

Start counting the day AFTER you serve the notice, and exclude weekends and legal holidays. So a notice served on Friday counts Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday — the notice expires Wednesday evening. This is stricter than most states so count carefully.

Can I include late fees in the Florida notice?

No — this is a critical Florida-specific rule. The notice must state only the exact rent amount due, nothing else. Including late fees, utility charges, or any other amounts invalidates the notice and forces you to start over.

What if I have a week-to-week tenant in Florida?

Week-to-week tenants receive a 7-day notice instead of 3 days. Month-to-month tenants get the standard 3-day pay or vacate notice for non-payment of rent.

Do I need to go to court after the notice expires?

Yes — if the tenant hasn't paid or vacated after the notice period, you must file a complaint for eviction with your county court. Florida does not allow self-help evictions under any circumstances.
