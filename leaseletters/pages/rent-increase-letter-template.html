What a Rent Increase Letter Must Include

  • ✓Tenant's full name and current property address
  • ✓Current monthly rent amount clearly stated
  • ✓New monthly rent amount and the exact effective date
  • ✓Required notice period — the letter must be delivered with enough advance notice per your state's law
  • ✓Instructions for response — whether the tenant must sign and return anything, or simply pay the new amount by the effective date
  • ✓Landlord signature and contact information

Tone: Firm but Professional

A rent increase letter doesn't need to be cold or adversarial. Acknowledging the tenant's tenancy, briefly noting the reason for the increase (rising costs, market rates, property improvements), and maintaining a respectful tone significantly reduces pushback and the chance a tenant decides to move out.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I raise rent mid-lease?

Generally no — a fixed-term lease locks in the rent for the duration unless the lease specifically includes a rent increase clause. You can only raise rent when the lease is up for renewal or when a tenant is on a month-to-month agreement.

Is there a maximum amount I can raise rent?

In most states, no — landlords can raise rent to market rate. However, in rent-controlled jurisdictions (New York City, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Portland, etc.), there are strict annual caps. Always check local ordinances before sending a large increase.

Does a rent increase letter need to be in writing?

Yes, in virtually every state. A verbal agreement to raise rent is nearly impossible to enforce. Written notice protects both parties and establishes a clear record. Keep a copy of everything you send.

What if the tenant refuses to pay the new rent?

If proper notice was given and the effective date has passed, a tenant paying the old amount is technically in partial non-payment of rent. You can treat the difference as unpaid rent and follow your state's non-payment procedures. Document everything from the start.
