Cure Periods: How Much Time Must You Give?

Most states require you to give the tenant a specific number of days to "cure" (fix) the violation before you can proceed with eviction. This varies by state and violation type:

  • 3California, Florida, Arizona — 3-day cure or quit for most violations. Some states allow longer for first-time offenses.
  • 10Washington, Oregon, Colorado — 10-day cure period for most lease violations before eviction proceedings can begin.
  • 14New York, Georgia, Michigan — 14-day cure period required, giving the tenant two weeks to remedy the situation.
  • 30Some violations in all states — Certain complex violations (like unauthorized alterations) may warrant a longer cure period. Use your judgment and document the reasoning.

⚠️ Some violations cannot be cured. In most states, certain serious violations — drug activity, criminal behavior, or a second occurrence of the same violation within a set period — allow you to issue an unconditional quit notice with no cure period. Consult an attorney before issuing these.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I talk to the tenant before sending a formal notice?

For minor first-time violations, a conversation can resolve things quickly and preserve the relationship. However, always follow up verbal discussions with a written notice — it creates documentation. If the issue is serious or recurring, go straight to written notice.

What if the tenant cures the violation but then repeats it?

Document each instance carefully. Many states allow landlords to issue an unconditional quit notice (no opportunity to cure) on a second or third occurrence of the same violation within a specified period, often 6 or 12 months. Keep copies of every notice you've sent.

Can I charge a fee for lease violations?

Only if your lease agreement specifically includes a violation fee clause and local law permits it. Even then, the fee must be reasonable. Never include a penalty fee in an eviction notice — it can invalidate the notice in some states.

Do I need to send the notice certified mail?

It's strongly recommended even when not required. Certified mail creates a delivery record that is very difficult for a tenant to dispute in court. For serious violations that may lead to eviction, it's essentially mandatory for your own protection.
