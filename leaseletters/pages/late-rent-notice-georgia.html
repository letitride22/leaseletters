How to Serve a Late Rent Notice in Georgia

Georgia law (Georgia Code Section 44-7-50) governs how notices must be delivered. Accepted methods include:

  • 1Personal service — Hand the notice directly to the tenant. This is the most reliable method and starts the clock immediately.
  • 2Certified mail — Send via USPS certified mail with return receipt. Keep the receipt as proof of delivery. Note that the notice period may not begin until the tenant receives it.
  • 3Posting and mailing — Post the notice in a conspicuous place on the property AND mail a copy the same day. Both steps are required for this method to be valid.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is written notice actually required in Georgia?

Technically Georgia law does not require a written notice before filing a dispossessory action — but it is strongly recommended. A written demand for possession creates documentation, gives the tenant a clear deadline, and demonstrates good faith if the case goes to court.

How fast is the Georgia eviction process?

Georgia is one of the fastest states for eviction. After the notice period, you file a Dispossessory Affidavit with the Magistrate Court. The tenant has 7 days to answer. If they don't, you can get a writ of possession within days. The entire process can take 2-3 weeks.

Can Georgia cities have rent control?

No — Georgia state law preempts local rent control ordinances, similar to Arizona. No city in Georgia can enact rent control, making it consistently landlord-friendly across the state.

What if the tenant abandons the property without paying?

Georgia has specific abandoned property rules. If the tenant leaves personal belongings, you cannot simply dispose of them. You must follow Georgia's abandoned property statute, which requires notice and a waiting period before disposal.
