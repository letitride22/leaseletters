How to Serve a Late Rent Notice in California

California Civil Code Section 1161 governs how notices must be delivered. You have three options:

  • 1Personal service — Hand the notice directly to the tenant. This is the most straightforward method and starts the 3-day clock immediately.
  • 2Substituted service — If the tenant isn't home, leave the notice with a person of suitable age at the property AND mail a copy to the tenant the same day.
  • 3Posting and mailing — If no one is home, post the notice on the main entry door AND mail a copy. The 3-day period doesn't begin until after the mailing date.

⚠️ Do not slide the notice under the door — this is not valid service in California and a tenant can use it to invalidate your eviction proceeding.

What Your Notice Must Include

A valid California 3-day pay or quit notice must contain all of the following:

  • ✓Tenant's full name and names of any other occupants
  • ✓Exact property address including unit number
  • ✓Exact amount owed — rent only, not late fees (unless your lease specifies otherwise)
  • ✓The 3-day deadline to pay or vacate
  • ✓Where and how to pay — name, address, and hours payment will be accepted
  • ✓Landlord or agent signature

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I charge late fees on a California late rent notice?

Only if your lease agreement specifically includes a late fee clause. Even then, the 3-day notice should clearly separate the base rent amount from any fees. Some landlords send the notice for rent only and pursue late fees separately to avoid complications.

Do weekends count toward the 3-day period?

Yes — under California law, weekends and holidays do count toward the 3-day notice period. However, if the last day falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day.

What if the tenant pays after receiving the notice?

If the tenant pays the full amount owed before the 3-day period expires, the notice is satisfied and you cannot proceed with eviction for that instance. Keep a record of payment. If they're habitually late, you can still document the pattern.

Does AB 1482 (California rent control) affect late rent notices?

AB 1482 primarily governs rent increase limits and just-cause eviction requirements for covered properties built before 2005. It doesn't change the notice requirements for late rent, but does affect your ability to proceed with eviction if the property is covered under just-cause protections.
